1.0
TO BEGIN
Images, signs, commodities, representations, are not only imperceptible to, but have become reality. Objects, the transformation of raw material through real human labour, flurry by as numbers, as values, severing the hands of its makers, or denying the hands ever existed at all. Your mind wonders to an escape, like what you’ve experienced before. Years ago. Don't you remember?
I think I’ve found it.
1.1.
WHEN THE CUTE BEGS, WE BUY
Cute is soft. Passive. Squishy. Pliable. Vulnerable. Infinitely forgiving. It greets you as its mother, begging for warmth. It’s doe-eyed, and submissive, it whimpers, “Please take care of me”. Perhaps we can exist in this shelter together, you think to yourself. Oh, to be a child again. To relish in a favourite blanket or teddy bear, perhaps tattered or grubby, completely innocent, completely untainted by monetary value. Simply soothing. Innocuous.
“BY THIS LOGIC, THE EPITOME
OF THE CUTE WOULD BE AN UNDIFFERENTIATED BLOB OF SOFT
DOUGHY MATTER.”1
1. Sianne Ngai, Our Aesthetic Categories: Zany, Cute, Interesting. [Harvard University Press, 2012], 64.
1.2.
A WARM AND
FUZZY FEELING
The cute commodity seems to take on a fantastic quality, akin to that of the Magic Puppy children’s books, where the pet of one lucky child suddenly converses to only them. So you stare at your pet, or as perhaps you do now, stare at your cute things, imagining a relationship, a connection, that if I just believe hard enough, the fantasy will be real. One, of course, animate, the other illusory in its aliveness. Or perhaps you think to your childhood imaginary friend, an imagination so powerful it supersedes reality. An in-built comfort.

2.1.
NOSTALGIA
AS COERCIVE
It is powerful to offer this nostalgia, this escapism, on demand. That by purchasing, for example (and topical, as of 2025), a Sonny Angel, or a Labubu, you can deny commodity fetishism, deny its essence of exchange-value, its reduction to the dollar sign. It is coercive, an empty promise of use value, of intimacy, or of shelter from capitalistic pressures.
“The cute object's exaggerated passivity seems likely to excite the consumer's sadism or desire for mastery
as much as
her desire to protect and cuddle.”2
2. Sianne Ngai, Our Aesthetic Categories: Zany, Cute, Interesting. [Harvard University Press, 2012], 65.
2.2.
CUTENESS
AS A PACIFIER
3.

These commodities embody an aestheticisation of powerlessness – where its physical soft qualities elicit an “imbalance of power between herself and the object,”3 going as far as to impress upon the subject a maternal impulse—the product has its own desires that we can fulfil, or care for (“The cute commodity flatteringly seems to want us and only us as its mommy”1).

The childlike nature of these commodities, the “dumbing down”4 of goods, induces psychological and behavioral regression. This infantilisation is all in the acceleration of capital – the child is “the ideal consumer because they are emotional, egocentric, and impulsive”4. It is employed as an ideological pacifier to an audience that is increasingly cynical, alienated, and in desperate desire for relief.

3.1.
POWER IN
CUTE'S CLOTHING
5.
Alan Moore, in an interview with IndieWire. [2022].
It is not the victims, the working class, to be blamed. Deeply complex, targeted mirages are designed to destabilise, to refocus masses on banalit ies. As capitalism pursues its insatiable growth, it adapts to consume. When the consumer no longer 'buys' (figuratively and literally) a particular facade, it burrows deeper, using ideological signifiers to latch onto the 'real'. Cuteness has been used to, and will continue to, conceal power.

It is not surprising that we have seen Sonny Angels or Labubus (or whatever the next trending 'cutie' is) soar in popularity (as part of a larger rise in ‘soft power’), with the inconspicuous ideology of infantilisation as a positive affect. That you deserve regression into comfort under capitalism, buy this commodity for shelter (but also to keep the cogs turning, so you keep buying more stuff). However, as Alan Moore warns, “that kind of infantilisation – that urge towards simpler times, simpler realities – that can very often be a precursor to fascism”5.
“the cute commodity, for all its pathos of powerlessness, is thus capable of making suprisingly powerful demands.”1
1. Sianne Ngai, Our Aesthetic Categories: Zany, Cute, Interesting. [Harvard University Press, 2012], 64.
3.2.
SEDUCTION
IS NOT
PASSIVE
As Ngai descibes, “for all its pathos of powerlessness the cute is able
to make surprisingly powerful demands”1. The cute commodity belies passivity, and induces infantilisation, whilst maintaining the contradictory illusion that consumption is an escape from capitalism, rather than its infrastructure. It is a political tool that is inconspicuously seductive. In creating the illusion of consumption as a liberating action, it conceals and works against the subject’s capacity for effective resistance that the infantilisation of the subject “is not such a bad way (to start with) of disposing of them”5. It acts to politically disarm, but we are not powerless, and are more perceptive than given credit to.

As fascism sharpens its blade, we cannot afford to fall victim to the mirage of its spectacle, even if the knife has a bow on it.
